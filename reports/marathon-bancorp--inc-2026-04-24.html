Verdict: Marathon Bancorp is a well-capitalized but marginally profitable community thrift with exceptional asset quality and a dangerously high loan-to-deposit ratio. The near-breakeven earnings profile and elevated efficiency ratio temper the appeal of an otherwise clean balance sheet. At current prices near 0.93x TBV, valuation is reasonable, but the earnings engine needs significant improvement before this becomes a compelling buy.
This is a classic small-town Wisconsin thrift — conservative, clean credit, overcapitalized post-conversion, but struggling to generate meaningful earnings. The 119% loan-to-deposit ratio is the most glaring structural concern; the bank is funding loans with FHLB borrowings rather than core deposits, which compresses NIM and adds interest rate/liquidity risk. The NIM at ~2.8% is below the 3% threshold and reflects both the funding cost pressure and a likely asset-sensitive balance sheet still adjusting post-rate-cycle. The efficiency ratio in the 85-90% range is characteristic of a sub-$250M bank with a fixed overhead base — every dollar of revenue growth flows disproportionately to the bottom line, making expense leverage the key investment thesis. The 15.27% Tier 1 leverage ratio is a genuine differentiator and provides significant capital optionality for a buyback, special dividend, or bolt-on acquisition, which is the classic second-step conversion playbook. Investors willing to underwrite a 2-3 year earnings normalization story may find value here, but the single-market concentration in Wausau and the razor-thin current profitability leave little margin for error.
